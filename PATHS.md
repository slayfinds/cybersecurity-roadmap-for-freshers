# Which Path Is Right For You?

Cybersecurity isn't one job - it's a family of fairly different careers that happen to share the word "security." Here's a more honest comparison than most "top 10 cybersecurity careers" articles give you.

## The four main entry-friendly paths

| | SOC / Blue Team | VAPT / Red Team | Network Security | GRC / Compliance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Day-to-day feel** | Monitor alerts, investigate, escalate, document | Find and exploit weaknesses, write reports | Design/maintain secure infra, firewalls, VPNs | Policy, audits, risk assessments, talking to stakeholders |
| **Best fit if you like** | Structured work, patterns, investigation | Puzzles, CTFs, "how would I break this" thinking | Infrastructure, networking, building things right | Process, writing, cross-team communication |
| **Coding required** | Light (some scripting helps) | Moderate-heavy (scripting is core) | Light-moderate | Minimal |
| **Typical time to job-ready** | 6-9 months | 8-12 months | 6-9 months | 4-6 months |
| **Entry-level title (India)** | SOC Analyst L1 | Junior Pentester / VAPT Analyst | Network Security Engineer (Jr) | GRC Analyst / Compliance Analyst |
| **How proof-of-skill is judged** | Lab writeups, SIEM familiarity | CTF rank, HTB/THM profile, writeups | Lab configs, network design exercises | Frameworks knowledge, communication ability |

## Honest take, not hype

- **SOC** is usually the *fastest* door in for most newcomers, and a very common stepping stone - many SOC analysts move into VAPT, threat hunting, or detection engineering later.
- **VAPT** is the most "glamorous" on social media but is genuinely harder to break into without strong, sustained CTF practice. Don't pick it just because it sounds cool - pick it if you actually enjoy the grind of CTFs.
- **Network Security** is underrated and plays directly to anyone who already has some networking/infra interest or background. Often less competitive at entry level than VAPT.
- **GRC** is the most accessible if you're stronger at communication/writing than deep technical work, and it pays well - but it's the least "hands-on-keyboard" of the four, which matters if that's what drew you to security in the first place.

## You don't have to choose forever

Most people's first security job is **not** their permanent specialization. A SOC Analyst L1 role, in particular, is a great place to figure out - with real exposure - whether you actually enjoy the offensive side (move to VAPT), the infra side (move to Network Security), or just go deeper into detection/IR.

If you're still unsure: **start with SOC fundamentals** ([`ROADMAP-SOC.md`](./ROADMAP-SOC.md)) regardless of your end goal - the foundational knowledge (networking, logs, common attacks) underlies all four paths, and SOC roles are the easiest entry point to get real, paid experience while you figure out the rest.
