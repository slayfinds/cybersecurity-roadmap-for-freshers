# Roadmap: GRC / Compliance Path

Estimated time: **4-6 months**. This is generally the fastest of the four paths to become job-ready for, because it leans more on understanding frameworks, process, and communication than deep hands-on technical skill - though a baseline of technical literacy is still required.

Good fit if you: like policy, writing, structured documentation, talking to stakeholders, and "why" questions more than "how" questions. Less hands-on-keyboard than SOC, VAPT, or Network Security.

---

## Phase 0: Foundations (2-3 weeks)

- [ ] Basic security concepts: CIA triad, common threats, defense in depth (you don't need deep technical depth, but you need to speak the language)
- [ ] Basic understanding of IT infrastructure: networks, applications, cloud, data flows - enough to read a system diagram and ask sensible questions
- [ ] Strong written communication - GRC work is largely documentation, reports, and policy writing

**Checkpoint:** Can you explain, in plain English, why a company would care about access control even if it never had a breach? If not, start there.

---

## Phase 1: Frameworks and standards (6-8 weeks)

This is the technical core of GRC work - knowing these frameworks well is what separates a credible candidate from someone reciting buzzwords.

- [ ] **ISO 27001** - the most commonly referenced security management standard globally, very common in India for client-facing roles
- [ ] **NIST CSF** (Cybersecurity Framework) - widely used, especially for US-facing clients
- [ ] **SOC 2** - extremely common requirement for SaaS/IT services companies, especially ones serving US clients
- [ ] **PCI DSS** basics - if your target companies touch payment data
- [ ] India-specific: **DPDP Act (Digital Personal Data Protection Act)** and basic GDPR awareness if you'll work with companies serving EU clients

**Portfolio item:** Pick one framework (ISO 27001 is a good start) and write a plain-language explainer of its structure - control domains, what an audit actually checks, common gaps companies have.

---

## Phase 2: Risk and audit fundamentals (6-8 weeks)

- [ ] Risk assessment basics: likelihood x impact, risk registers, risk treatment (accept/mitigate/transfer/avoid)
- [ ] Understand the audit lifecycle: scoping, evidence collection, gap assessment, remediation tracking, certification/attestation
- [ ] Policy vs procedure vs standard vs guideline - know the difference, you'll be writing these
- [ ] Third-party/vendor risk management basics - increasingly important as companies outsource more

**Portfolio item:** Build a sample risk register for a hypothetical small company (5-10 example risks, with likelihood/impact scoring and treatment plans). This is a very standard interview exercise - having one ready is a real advantage.

---

## Phase 3: Hands-on practice (6-8 weeks)

- [ ] Write a sample security policy (e.g. Access Control Policy or Acceptable Use Policy) from scratch using a public template as a base, then customize it
- [ ] Do a mock internal audit against a checklist (ISO 27001 Annex A controls are a good practice set) - even on a hypothetical or your own home setup
- [ ] Practice gap analysis: compare a fictional company's current state against a framework's requirements, document the gaps

**Portfolio item:** Put together a small "audit package" - a checklist, your findings (fictional but realistic), and a remediation plan. This demonstrates you understand the full cycle, not just the theory.

---

## Phase 4: Tools awareness (parallel, lighter than other paths)

- [ ] GRC platforms exist (Vanta, Drata, OneTrust, ServiceNow GRC) - you don't need hands-on access necessarily, but know what they do and why companies use them
- [ ] Spreadsheet fluency (Excel/Sheets) - a huge amount of real GRC work happens in well-organized spreadsheets before it ever touches a fancy platform
- [ ] Basic familiarity with documentation/collaboration tools (Jira, Confluence) used to track audits and remediation

---

## Phase 5: Certification

1. **ISO 27001 Lead Implementer or Internal Auditor** - accessible, well-regarded, a strong first cert for this path
2. **CompTIA Security+** - still useful as a baseline technical credential even in GRC roles
3. Later, once experienced: **CISA** (Certified Information Systems Auditor) or **CRISC** - these typically require some years of experience to even sit for, so they're a later-career target, not a starting point

---

## Phase 6: Applying

- [ ] Target roles: **GRC Analyst**, **Compliance Analyst**, **IT Audit Associate**, **Information Security Analyst (Compliance focus)**
- [ ] Documentation discipline, process-following, and clear stakeholder communication are exactly what this role needs - highlight any experience that demonstrates these, even from unrelated fields
- [ ] Highlight any experience writing SOPs, handling audits, or working with compliance-adjacent processes, even informally

## A realistic note

GRC is sometimes dismissed as "less technical" or a lesser path, which is unfair - it pays well, is in steady demand (every company doing business with the US or EU needs this), and is a genuinely good fit if your strengths lean toward communication and structure rather than deep hands-on technical work. Don't pick it by default because it seems "easier" though - pick it because the day-to-day actually appeals to you.
